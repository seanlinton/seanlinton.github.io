Opinions on amount of responsibility for different groups

These graphs show what people think about the amount of responsibility that different groups: individuals, communities, corporations, and governments have when it comes to causing and reducing climate change.

We can see that across the board, people think that all of these groups are not doing enough for climate change. It is evident that the general opinion is that governments and corporations are not doing enough, even more so than individuals.

This is important to note as everyone needs to be doing what they can to help climate change. However, the amount of work put in needs to reflect the relative damage caused.

This is not saying that individuals should simply do nothing, rather they should do what is in their power. And if their responsibility is not within their power, action needs to be taken in order to get the necessary resources and capabilities to do so.

* Data collected from our research survey

Electricity consumption between Individual and Big Corporations

The average U.S. household consumes around 1,000 kilowatt-hours (kWh) of electricity each month, or 12 megawatt-hours (MWh) per year.

Here is the bar chart that visualizes the electricity consumption of the tech companies compared to the average U.S. household. Each bar represents the equivalent number of U.S. households that would consume the same amount of electricity as the respective company does annually.

As shown, Samsung's consumption is equivalent to the annual electricity use of about 2.36 million average U.S. households, which is the highest among the listed companies. Alphabet (Google) and Microsoft are also significantly high, with consumptions equivalent to around 1.52 million and 1.51 million households, respectively.

This visualization underscores the substantial electricity consumption by these tech companies when compared to the average household.

* Data collected from Statista. "Electricity Consumption among Leading Tech Companies Worldwide in 2022, by Company (in 1,000 Megawatt Hours)." Statista, Statista Inc., 24 Jul 2023, https://www.statista.com/statistics/1250731/electricity-consumption-top-tech-companies-worldwide/

CO2 Emissions between Individual and Big Corporations

According to the EPA, the average four-person household produces 83,000 pounds of greenhouse gas emissions per year, or 20,750 pounds per person. This is equivalent to 7.5 tons of CO2-equivalents per year.

Here is the bar chart visualizing the greenhouse gas emissions of the tech companies compared to the average U.S. household. Each bar represents the equivalent number of U.S. households that would produce the same amount of greenhouse gas emissions as the respective company does annually.

Samsung's emissions are equivalent to those of about 158,626 U.S. households, which is significantly higher than any other company listed. This could reflect the impact of Samsung's extensive manufacturing operations.

This visualization serves as a compelling representation of the carbon footprint of major tech companies and provides a perspective on the scale of emissions in comparison to individual household contributions.

*Data collected from Statista. "Scope 1 Greenhouse Gas Emissions from Leading Tech Companies Worldwide in 2022, by Company (in Metric Tons of Co2 Equivalent)." Statista, Statista Inc., 24 Jul 2023, https://www.statista.com/statistics/1250461/greenhouse-gas-emissions-scope-1-top-tech-companies-worldwide/

Sources of stress

The chart indicates that the most significant source of pressure to protect the climate comes from 'Self-Driven' motives, suggesting that personal values and beliefs are the primary motivators for individuals. This could imply a strong individual recognition of climate issues and personal responsibility towards environmental stewardship.

'Communities' and 'Corporations' are also notable sources of pressure, indicating a societal push towards climate action. This might reflect the growing community-driven initiatives and the increasing role of corporate social responsibility in driving sustainable practices.

'Government' seems to be a less significant source of pressure compared to the other categories, which could suggest that individuals may not feel as compelled by government actions or policies, or it could reflect a perception that government initiatives are insufficient or not as directly influential on individual actions.

The 'Other' category has the least impact, which is typical as it often serves as a catch-all for less common responses not covered by the main categories.

Renewable Energy Usage

Apple, Meta, Alphabet (Google), and Microsoft have achieved a 100% renewable energy usage in their operations.

Intel is close behind with 93% renewable energy usage, suggesting significant steps toward sustainability, though not quite at the full 100% mark.

NVIDIA and QUALCOMM show moderate levels of renewable energy usage at 44% and 34%, respectively. While these figures show a commitment to renewable energy, they also highlight the potential for improvement and further transition.

We can see that renewable energy usage in households is quite low.

*Data collected from Statista. "Share of Renewable Energy Used among Leading Tech Companies Worldwide in 2022, by Company." Statista, Statista Inc., 9 Nov 2023, https://www.statista.com/statistics/1250742/renewable-energy-top-tech-companies-worldwide/

Project Context

Thank you for interacting with our display! We are a group of students at the University of Pittsburgh attempting to promote awareness about and reflection on the occurrence of responsibilization in climate change.

Responsibilization is the transfer of accountability and responsibility of various issues from a higher authority (e.g. corporations, governments) to a lower authority (e.g. individuals) whom now needs to solve those issues.

Through displaying data obtained in our own research, as well as data obtained from other sources, we explore the line of differences in how groups of different levels of power contribute to and resolve climate change.
